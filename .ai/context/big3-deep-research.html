Can We Register "Big3" in Classes 9 & 42?

✓ Favorable Precedent: Identical Marks Coexist Across Classes

US trademark law allows identical marks in different classes when there's no "likelihood of confusion." Well-established examples:

  • Delta — Airlines (Class 39) vs. Faucets (Class 11). Same exact word, zero confusion.
  • Dove — Soap (Class 3) vs. Chocolate/Ice Cream (Class 30). Both consumer products, still coexist.
  • Pandora — Jewelry (Class 14) vs. Music streaming (Class 38/41). Both consumer-facing, still coexist.
  • Apple — Computers (Class 9) vs. Records (Class 9/41). Famously coexisted for decades with agreement.

Likelihood of Confusion Analysis (DuPont Factors)

The USPTO uses a multi-factor test to determine if two marks would confuse consumers. Here's how Big3 (astrology app) vs. BIG3 (basketball league) scores:

Factor Analysis Risk
Similarity of marks Identical word. However, stylization and context differ. High
Similarity of goods/services Basketball entertainment vs. astrology software. Completely unrelated. Low
Trade channels Sports venues/TV vs. App Store/web. Zero overlap. Low
Consumer sophistication Sports fans vs. astrology enthusiasts. Distinct demographics. Low
Fame of prior mark BIG3 Basketball is moderately known (1M+ viewers, Ice Cube). Not a household name like Nike. Medium
Actual confusion evidence None exists (our product doesn't exist yet). "Big 3" is also a common astrology term. Low
Variety of similar marks in use Multiple "Big 3" entities exist across industries (consulting, packaging, auto, etc.) Low
⚠ The Key Risk Factor: "The Big 3 LLC" (Software Company)

While BIG3 Basketball is in completely different classes, The Big 3 LLC (thebig3llc.com) IS a software development company. If they have an active trademark in Class 9, that could be a closer conflict. However, our research shows they're a very small company with minimal presence and no visible USPTO trademark filing. Your attorney should verify this with a formal TESS search.

✓ "Big 3" as Astrology Term = Strong Descriptive Defense

In astrology, "Big 3" (Sun, Moon, Rising) is a universally recognized concept — used by AstroStyle, Tarot.com, Vice, Today.com, Astro-Seek, and virtually every astrology platform. This gives us a strong argument that the term is descriptive/generic within our industry. We're not borrowing their name — we're using our industry's own terminology.

BIG3 Basketball's Legal History

Important context — BIG3 Basketball has been involved in significant litigation, but NONE of it was about trademark/naming disputes:

  • $250M lawsuit from Champions Basketball League (player poaching, not trademark)
  • $1.2B lawsuit against Trinity investor (financial dispute)
  • $21M defamation judgment won by BIG3
  • Suit against Quinn Emanuel law firm (attorney-client dispute)

There is no history of BIG3 Basketball aggressively defending their trademark against companies in other industries. They've been focused on internal business disputes, not brand policing.

Trademark Registration Strategy

Recommended Approach:
  1. File Intent-to-Use (ITU) in Classes 9 & 42 — $350/class = $700 total
  2. Use full name: "BIG3" or "BIG3.ME" (using the domain as the mark adds distinctiveness)
  3. Description of services: "Downloadable mobile application for providing astrological charts, horoscopes, and cosmic weather forecasts" (Class 9) and "Software as a service featuring astrological analysis and personalized cosmic weather" (Class 42)
  4. Consider a coexistence agreement with BIG3 Basketball proactively — this eliminates future uncertainty and is common practice
  5. Hire trademark attorney for formal clearance search before filing — budget $1,500-2,500 for comprehensive search + opinion letter

⚠ The Hard Truth About "big3" as a Search Term

If someone searches just "big3" on Google, they will get BIG3 Basketball. Period. BIG3 Basketball owns big3.com, has a Wikipedia page, and has massive social media (Instagram @thebig3, X @thebig3). We will NEVER outrank them for the naked "big3" query. This is a significant brand discovery problem.

✓ The Good News: Our Target Keywords Are Different

Nobody searching "big 3 astrology" is looking for basketball. And nobody searching "big3 basketball" wants an astrology app. Google is very good at understanding intent. The astrology-specific long-tail keywords ("big 3 astrology app", "what's my big 3", "big 3 calculator", "cosmic weather") are WIDE OPEN and perfectly aligned with our product. We'd be building a new category: "Big 3 = astrology app" in addition to the existing "Big 3 = basketball."

SEO Strategy If We Go With Big3

🎯 Branded Search

Always promote as "big3.me" (with domain) not just "big3". The .me domain IS the brand differentiator. Users searching "big3.me" will find us instantly. Same strategy as cal.com or vercel.com — the domain IS the brand.

🔍 Long-Tail SEO

Target: "big 3 astrology app", "what's my big 3 sign", "big 3 calculator", "cosmic weather forecast", "transit intensity today". These queries have ZERO basketball competition.

📱 App Store Optimization

Apple App Store and Google Play are SEPARATE ecosystems from Google Search. "Big3" in the app store will compete with apps, not websites. BIG3 Basketball's app (Big3tv) is a streaming app — different category entirely.

🌐 Domain Authority

Build domain authority on big3.me through celebrity birth chart pages, "What's Your Big 3?" viral calculator, daily cosmic weather blog posts, and astrology education content.

6. Final Recommendation & Action Items

Verdict: GO — with conditions

Big3 is a strong brand name for an astrology app — possibly the most naturally viral name possible in this space. The trademark situation is favorable (different classes, different industry, established legal precedent for coexistence). The SEO challenge is real but manageable since our actual target keywords ("big 3 astrology", "cosmic weather", "what's my big 3") have zero basketball overlap.

The main trade-off is accepting that we'll never own the naked "big3" search query — but brands like The Pattern (competes with "pattern" = sewing, design, etc.) and Co-Star (competes with "co-star" = movies, TV) have proven this is manageable.
